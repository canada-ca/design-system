When to use: Certain colours should be used to convey meaning, usually to warn or indicate success.

When to avoid: Do not use meaningful colours out of context, except with the suggested palettes.

Usage

Don't

Colours should never be used exclusively to convey meaning.

Do

Match colours with textual cues and/or invisible text for screen readers.

Do

Particularly warning and success colours (reds and greens) should always have visible text distinctions, as these appear the same to those with colour blindness.

Usage

While you may use other colours, ensure that the primary content on the page uses the standard primary or secondary background. When you use other colours, ensure sufficient contrast with text to meet WCAG success criterion 1.4.6: Contrast (Enhanced) (Level AAA)

Colour contrast accessibility requirements

Do

Colour contrast for text must be at least 7:1 for normal text or 4.5:1 for large text (AAA).

Don't

Don't use the default text colours on custom surfaces.

Do

Use a colour contrast checker, such as to test any non-standard colours or colour combinations. Ensure that colours used are WCAG Level AAA.

Use low contrast ratios for text

Do

Dyslexic readers benefit from lower contrast ratios and pastel colour backgrounds (and comic sans). What does Canada.ca do or what could it do for dyslexic readers????

Supposedly 1 in 15 Canadians are dyslexic (unclear what research is behind that stat...).

Some publicly available research done around dyslexia and text, including colour combinations and contrast ratios.
